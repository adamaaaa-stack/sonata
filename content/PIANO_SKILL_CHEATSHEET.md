# Piano Skills Cheat Sheet — for Sonata's product lead (you)

You don't need to *play* these. You need to know the names and
why they matter, so you can plan lessons with Claude that cover them.

Organized from easiest to hardest. Every skill has:
- **What it is** (plain English)
- **What's actually hard about it** (the real problem students hit)
- **Which piece it unlocks** (why we teach it)

---

## 🟢 Beginner skills (lessons 1–75)

### 1. Hand independence
- **What:** Making your left and right hand do different things at the same time.
- **Hard because:** Your brain wants both hands to mirror. It takes reps to teach your body that the hands can separate.
- **Unlocks:** Any two-handed piece, even Happy Birthday with a simple LH chord.

### 2. Finger numbering / fingering
- **What:** Each finger gets a number (1=thumb, 5=pinky). Sheet music tells you which finger goes on which note.
- **Hard because:** Students ignore fingering and use whatever finger is closest. This kills them later — you can't play scales without the right fingering.
- **Unlocks:** Scales, smooth melodies, anything over 5 notes without jumping.

### 3. Reading rhythm
- **What:** Knowing how long each note lasts — quarter notes (1 beat), half notes (2 beats), whole notes (4 beats), eighth notes (half a beat).
- **Hard because:** Rhythm is a separate skill from pitch. Lots of beginners learn to name notes but can't keep a beat.
- **Unlocks:** Everything. Rhythm is the skeleton.

---

## 🟡 Elementary skills (lessons 26–75)

### 4. Reading both clefs at once (grand staff)
- **What:** The right hand reads the top staff (treble clef), the left hand reads the bottom (bass clef). You have to read both simultaneously.
- **Hard because:** Your eyes can't focus on two staves at the exact same time. You learn to scan up and down fast.
- **Unlocks:** Real sheet music. Before this, everything is just melody lines.

### 5. Scales
- **What:** Playing the notes of a key in order, smoothly, with the right fingers (including the "thumb under" move).
- **Hard because:** Thumb-under is physically awkward the first 50 times. Also: 12 major keys means 12 scales.
- **Unlocks:** Fast passages in any piece. Scales show up everywhere in classical music.

### 6. Dynamics (loud/soft control)
- **What:** Making some notes louder or softer on purpose. The markings are *p* (soft), *f* (loud), and a bunch in between.
- **Hard because:** Your hand defaults to even volume. Playing loud AND controlled is a real skill. Playing soft AND clear is even harder.
- **Unlocks:** Any piece that isn't boring. No dynamics = robot playing.

---

## 🟠 Intermediate skills (lessons 76–175)

### 7. Articulation (how you touch the keys)
- **What:** Legato = smooth, notes connected. Staccato = short, notes bouncy. Tenuto = held. Accent = punched.
- **Hard because:** Each one is a different physical motion of the finger/wrist/arm. Legato especially — keeping one note ringing until the next one starts — takes muscle memory.
- **Unlocks:** Bach (needs clear articulation), classical style in general.

### 8. The sustain pedal
- **What:** The rightmost foot pedal. You press it and notes ring out even after you lift your fingers. You change it in time with the music.
- **Hard because:** Pedal-and-hands coordination is like rubbing your belly and patting your head. Also: bad pedaling sounds muddy. Knowing *when not to pedal* is half the skill.
- **Unlocks:** Almost all Romantic-era music (Chopin, Debussy, Beethoven later works).

### 9. Chord voicing
- **What:** When you play 3-5 notes at once, you can make one of them louder than the others. That note becomes the "melody" and the rest are "accompaniment."
- **Hard because:** Your pinky (weakest finger) is usually the melody note. Making it louder than the stronger fingers takes real control.
- **Unlocks:** Anything that has chords with a tune on top — which is most music.

### 10. Triplets and complex rhythms
- **What:** Normally you count 1-and-2-and. Triplets are 1-trip-let 2-trip-let. Sixteenth notes are even faster.
- **Hard because:** Your foot taps in 2s or 4s naturally. Triplets fight that instinct.
- **Unlocks:** Moonlight Sonata 1st movement is ALL triplets. So you need this skill for a Tier 4 boss.

## 🔴 Upper-intermediate / Advanced skills (lessons 176–250)

### 11. Two-voice polyphony (counterpoint)
- **What:** Your right hand plays a melody. Your left hand plays a DIFFERENT melody. Not accompaniment — a real, separate tune. They happen at the same time and both matter equally.
- **Hard because:** You have to listen to both as full melodies. Most students' left hand goes mechanical and their right hand goes expressive. Bach demands the opposite: both hands expressive, independent.
- **Unlocks:** Bach Inventions, Fugues, most Baroque music. Also: real jazz.

### 12. Arpeggios at speed
- **What:** Rolling through the notes of a chord, fast and evenly, across big distances on the keyboard.
- **Hard because:** Your hand has to jump accurately without looking, while staying relaxed (tension makes you slow down).
- **Unlocks:** Moonlight 1st (slow arpeggios), Moonlight 3rd (fast arpeggios), all of Chopin.

### 13. Rubato
- **What:** Speeding up and slowing down *inside* a phrase on purpose, then giving the time back so the overall tempo is still correct.
- **Hard because:** It sounds fake if you do it wrong. It only works if the rest of your technique is strong enough that you can afford to stretch time.
- **Unlocks:** Chopin, Schumann, anything Romantic. This is the #1 thing that separates "technically good" from "musically alive."

### 14. Ornaments
- **What:** Tiny decorative notes — trills, grace notes, mordents, turns. Little flourishes around the main melody.
- **Hard because:** The symbols are cryptic. Also, Baroque ornaments have completely different rules than Romantic ornaments. Same symbol, different meaning.
- **Unlocks:** Bach (Baroque ornaments), Chopin (Romantic ornaments), any classical piece.

### 15. Presto technique (fast clean playing)
- **What:** Playing really fast without your hands getting tense, without notes blurring together, without losing evenness.
- **Hard because:** Tension is the enemy of speed. Most students fight the piano and their hands seize up. Professional pianists are extremely relaxed even at top speed.
- **Unlocks:** Moonlight Sonata 3rd movement. Also Liszt, Rachmaninoff, Chopin etudes.

## 🏔️ Specifically for Moonlight Sonata 3rd movement

This is your graduation piece (L250). Here's what it demands, in order of difficulty:

1. **Fast arpeggios** (skill #12) — the right hand is almost all 16th-note arpeggios
2. **Presto tempo** (skill #15) — the marking is *presto agitato* (very fast, agitated)
3. **Sudden loud-soft contrasts** (skill #6) — the *sforzando* accents are explosive
4. **Hand independence at speed** (skill #1) — while the RH storms, the LH has to stay rock-solid
5. **Pedaling at speed** (skill #8) — you pedal on every harmony change, which happens multiple times per bar
6. **Voicing inside arpeggios** (skill #9) — the top note of each arpeggio is the actual melody
7. **Endurance** — the whole movement is ~7 minutes of nonstop intensity

That's 7 skills layered together. That's why the course has 225 lessons building up to it.

## How to use this with Claude

When you're planning a lesson, you can say things like:

> "L58 should cover skill #6 (dynamics). Make it gentle. It's the first time we introduce it."

Or:

> "L225 is the last lesson before Advanced starts. It should be a drill combining skill #12 and skill #15. Keep it serious."

Or even just:

> "I'm planning L142. It's about ornaments (skill #14). Go."

You don't need to know *how* to teach it. You need to know *that* it needs teaching. This cheat sheet gives you the map.
