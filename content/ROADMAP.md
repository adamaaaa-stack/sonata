# Sonata · The Roadmap

The single source of truth for how a person goes from zero piano
to playing Moonlight Sonata 3rd movement using the Sonata method.

Every lesson, every design choice, every feature — checks against this document.
If something doesn't fit here, it doesn't ship.

---

## 1 · The promise

**Sonata takes someone who has never touched a piano and teaches them to
read and play sheet music — up to and including the most famous piano
pieces ever written — using one method: reading by shape, not by note name.**

The method is called the **Sonata method**. Its spine is three words:

> **Step · Skip · Leap**

Plus one rule that makes reading fast:

> **Odd / Even** — even intervals cross a line and a space (steps, 4ths, 6ths, octaves).
> Odd intervals stay on the same kind (skip = line→line or space→space; 5ths, 7ths).

That's the entire method. Everything else in the 250 lessons is scaffolding
around making those words instinctual.

---

## 2 · Who this is for

**Ages 10–45.** Absolute beginners. Zero piano experience.

- A 10-year-old can do this because Cleffy explains everything like a patient friend.
- A 45-year-old can do this because we don't talk down to them or cover it in cartoons.
- Both demographics care about the same three things: *am I making progress?*
  *am I being respected?* *will I actually be able to play real music?*
- The answer to all three has to be yes, every lesson.

**What they bring:** ears (trained by a lifetime of music), patience (limited),
curiosity (high), free time (15 min/day if we're lucky).

**What they do NOT bring:**
- Music theory vocabulary
- Any ability to read a staff
- A real piano in many cases (use the virtual keyboard)
- Patience for lectures

## 3 · The six transformations

The journey breaks into six tiers. Each tier is a transformation — a
sentence that starts with "Before this tier I couldn't…" and ends with
"…but now I can."

### Tier 1 · Beginner (L1–L25) — 25 lessons
> Before: I'd never touched a piano. Musical notation looks like scribbles.
> After: **I can play Happy Birthday from memory and from a page. I can hear when a
> melody goes up or down. I know what a step and a skip are.**

Anchor piece: **Happy Birthday**
Realistic time: **3–5 weeks** at 1 lesson/day.
The feeling at the end: *"Holy crap, I can play piano."*

### Tier 2 · Elementary (L26–L75) — 50 lessons
> Before: Only my right hand works. I know one tune.
> After: **Both hands together. I can read either clef. I know every white key. I've
> played Ode to Joy, Mary Had a Little Lamb, Jingle Bells, and I'm starting Für Elise.**

Anchor pieces: **Merrily We Roll Along · Mary Had a Little Lamb · Ode to Joy · Jingle Bells · Amazing Grace · Für Elise (theme)**
Realistic time: **2–3 months**.
The feeling at the end: *"I'm reading sheet music. Not fast, but really reading it."*

### Tier 3 · Low-Intermediate (L76–L125) — 50 lessons
> Before: I can play notes in order.
> After: **I know what chords are. I can play with different touches (legato, staccato).
> I can shape a phrase. I'm playing Minuet in G and the opening of Clair de Lune.**

Anchor pieces: **Minuet in G · Canon in D · Gymnopédie · Twinkle harmonized · Minuet in D · Bagatelle · Clair de Lune (opening)**
Realistic time: **2–3 months**.
The feeling at the end: *"Music has colors now. I hear the difference when I play softer."*

### Tier 4 · Intermediate (L126–L175) — 50 lessons
> Before: I play one key.
> After: **I play in 4+ keys. I know the sustain pedal. I can read triplets and sixteenths.
> I just played the first 8 bars of Moonlight Sonata.**

Anchor pieces: **Prelude in C · The Entertainer · Tarantella · Nocturne (phrase) · Chopin Waltz · Für Elise (full) · Moonlight 1st movement**
Realistic time: **3–4 months**.
The feeling at the end: *"I'm playing real classical music. I'm a pianist."*

### Tier 5 · Upper-Intermediate (L176–L225) — 50 lessons
> Before: One hand leads, one hand follows.
> After: **I play Bach counterpoint (two independent voices). I voice chords — I make the
> melody louder than the accompaniment, even when they're in the same hand. I've played a full
> Chopin Nocturne and all of Clair de Lune.**

Anchor pieces: **Bach Invention No. 1 · Nocturne Op. 9 No. 2 (full) · Chopin Prelude E minor · Schumann Träumerei · Debussy Arabesque · Grieg Arietta · Clair de Lune (full)**
Realistic time: **3–4 months**.
The feeling at the end: *"I'm playing pieces my music teacher wouldn't expect someone self-taught to be playing."*

### Tier 6 · Advanced (L226–L250) — 25 lessons (will likely grow to ~35)
> Before: I play real music, but slowly.
> After: **I'm working on Moonlight 3rd movement — a piece normally reserved for
> conservatory-level players — using everything I've learned to approach it honestly.**

Anchor pieces: **Fantaisie-Impromptu (middle) · Liszt Consolation No. 3 · Moonlight 3rd movement**
Realistic time: **2–4 months** (or longer — this is the top of the mountain).
The feeling at the end: *"I stopped asking 'can I?' and started asking 'how well?'"*

**Honest note on Moonlight 3rd:** This is a Diploma-level piece. Conservatoire students
spend years on it under teachers who watch their hands. Most Sonata users won't finish it —
and that's not failure. Tier 4 ending with Moonlight 1st movement is already a life-changing
outcome for someone who started 12 months ago with zero experience. Every tier is a real win.
See the "every tier is a win" section below.

### Every tier is a real win (new)

Most users will not reach Tier 6. That is fine. The marketing, the in-app messaging,
and Cleffy's voice all need to make each tier feel like a complete story, not a
waypoint toward a far-off goal.

| You finished... | You can say... |
|---|---|
| Tier 1 | "I played Happy Birthday at a party. I can read a staff." |
| Tier 2 | "I play Für Elise. I read both clefs. I use both hands." |
| Tier 3 | "I play Minuet in G, Gymnopédie, and the opening of Clair de Lune." |
| Tier 4 | "I play Moonlight Sonata 1st movement." |
| Tier 5 | "I play a full Chopin Nocturne and all of Clair de Lune." |
| Tier 6 | "I'm working on Moonlight 3rd. The summit." |

Churn at Tier 3 or 4 should not feel like failure — those are genuine achievements.
The lifetime subscription makes this honest: buy once, stop whenever, still keep
access to everything for practice and repertoire.

### Flexible tier sizing (new)

The six tiers of "50 lessons each" is a planning approximation, not a contract.
Real pedagogy isn't evenly weighted. Tier 2 (both hands + bass clef + dynamics +
accidentals + first key signatures + phrasing) is heavier than Tier 3. Tier 6
(Moonlight 3rd) needs more room than 25 lessons. Expect the final curriculum to
land around **~260 lessons**, not exactly 250, with individual tiers ranging
from 25 to 60 depending on content weight. The marketing number stays "~250."

### Total journey
- Conservative: **14–20 months** at 1 lesson/day
- Aggressive: **10–14 months** at 2 lessons/day
- If you stop at Tier 3 or 4, you're a pianist. No one checks whether you
  reached the summit.

## 4 · The skill graph — what depends on what

Teaching order isn't arbitrary. These are the dependencies. If you try
to teach something before its parent, the student won't get it.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  HEARING (L1)                                          │
│  Up/down · loud/soft · fast/slow · step/skip/leap      │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                       ↓
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  PIANO GEOGRAPHY (L2–L10)                              │
│  High/low → black-key map → C → F → alphabet → octaves │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                       ↓
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STAFF GEOGRAPHY (L11–L15)                             │
│  Five lines → treble clef → Middle C                   │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                       ↓
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE METHOD (L16–L25)                                  │
│  Step → odd/even → skip → mix → rhythm → 4/4 → piece   │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                       ↓
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  BOTH HANDS (L26–L50)                                  │
│  Bass clef → LH reading → grand staff → leaps          │

                       ↓
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
```

## 6 · The Sonata method — stated as rules

These are **non-negotiable**. Any lesson that violates one of these isn't a Sonata lesson.

1. **Shape before name.** Students see a step, skip, or leap *before* they learn the note names. Names are labels for shapes they already recognize.
2. **No mnemonics.** FACE, Every Good Boy, All Cows Eat Grass — banned. If a student memorizes "FACE," they've quit the method. Cleffy derives every note from Middle C + intervals.
3. **Ears before eyes.** Every new concept is heard before it's seen. Lesson 1 is all listening.
4. **Every note derives from an anchor.** Middle C → other treble notes. Bass F → other bass notes. Not brute memorization.
5. **The method vocabulary never changes.** Step, skip, leap, odd, even — used from L1 to L250. No substitute words.
6. **Intervals, not pitches, from day one.** We don't ask "what note is this?" first. We ask "is this a step or a skip from the last note?"
7. **Reading corrections use method language.** *"You picked leap but it's a skip — these notes are on different lines but only one line apart,"* never *"wrong, it's E."*

## 7 · Where students will hit walls

Five places in the 250 lessons where people quit if we don't handle them
carefully. Each needs extra care in authoring.

### Wall 1 — First sight of the staff (L11)
The moment the abstract "music is stairs" becomes "music is this weird
5-line drawing." We softened this by introducing the staff literally AS
a drawing of the stairs (L13). Must still be gentle. Authoring rule: no
note names on L11. Just shape.

### Wall 2 — Two hands at once (L33–L50)
Physical brain challenge. Most people's first attempts are awful. We
help by:
- Starting with hands asymmetric (RH melody + LH holds one note)
- Teaching each hand separately first, together last
- Lots of remediation for this act

### Wall 3 — First key signature with sharps (L67)
"Wait, now F is F-sharp EVERY TIME? I have to remember that?" We handle by:
- Visual highlight of every F on the keyboard showing which are now black
- One-sharp key only at first (G major)
- Lots of repetition before adding a second key

### Wall 4 — The pedal (L156–L160)
Coordination nightmare. Foot moves with harmony changes, not with notes.
We teach by:
- Pedal-only drills (no keys played)
- Pedal on chord changes only, holding hands still
- Slowly adding hands

### Wall 5 — Moonlight 3 speed (L238–L246)
The actual boss. We've spent 237 lessons building up. We handle by:
- Bars 1–4 at 30% tempo, 50%, 75%, 100% over four separate lessons
- Never combine "new bars" with "new speed" in the same lesson
- Full graduation is 24 pages (the only lesson that long)

## 8 · Mastery & gating — the don't-skip rule

Every lesson ends with a **mastery check** (4–6 questions across SEE/HEAR/PLAY).
If the student doesn't pass, they don't unlock the next lesson.

**But failure ≠ repeat the same lesson.** Failure routes to a **remediation
lesson** (numbered `.5`) — shorter, slower, different explanation.

Remediation variants:
- `.5a` — failed SEE section → more visual examples
- `.5b` — failed HEAR section → more audio, slower
- `.5c` — failed PLAY section → guided hand/finger placement
- `.5` (no letter) — failed 2+ sections → full re-teach with a different metaphor

After any remediation, they return to the mastery check. Can't
progress without passing. Cleffy is always kind about it:
> *"Hey. No big deal. Ears are like muscles — they just need a few reps."*

**Every 5 lessons (act boss)** → cumulative check across the act.
**Every 25–50 lessons (tier boss)** → performance of an anchor piece.
**Tier 5 & 6 bosses** → recording feature (future) so they can share.

## 9 · The listening track

Ear training runs through every lesson. Not a separate "ear training"
feature — a required part of every lesson's HEAR section.

By lesson 50: student hears step vs skip vs leap by ear.
By lesson 100: student hears intervals (3rd vs 5th vs octave).
By lesson 150: student hears scales vs arpeggios vs chords as textures.
By lesson 200: student hears counterpoint (two melodies at once).
By lesson 250: student hears a piece and can describe its shape without the page.

## 10 · Cleffy's voice — locked

From Lesson 1 we set the tone. From now on every lesson matches it.

**Do:**
- Short sentences.
- Contractions ("you'll," "that's").
- Occasional "right?" / "watch this" / "here's the trick."
- Respect 10-year-olds AND 45-year-olds. No baby talk, no textbook.
- Warmth. Patience. Curious energy. Bob Ross for music.

**Don't:**
- No emoji in Cleffy's speech (they appear only on stickers/rewards).
- No exclamation-point spam. Max one per page.
- No fake hype. "AMAZING JOB!!!" is banned.
- No jargon before it's taught.
- No over-explaining. If it's obvious, move on.

Cleffy knows you can do this. He's never worried. He's patient. When you
miss, he assumes the lesson didn't land, not that you're dumb.

## 11 · Time map for the student

Progress realistically looks like this. This goes in the app somewhere
so students see progress isn't a race.

| Pace | Lessons/day | Tier 1 done | Tier 3 done | Tier 5 done | Graduation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Casual | 0.5 (every other day) | ~7 weeks | ~7 months | ~20 months | ~2.5 years |
| Steady | 1 | ~4 weeks | ~4 months | ~10 months | **~14 months** |
| Focused | 2 | ~2 weeks | ~2 months | ~5 months | ~7 months |
| Unhealthy | 4+ | 1 week | 1 month | 3 months | 4 months |

**The headline: 1 lesson/day for 14 months = you play Moonlight Sonata.**

Casual and unhealthy are included to be honest. The unhealthy pace skips
too much practice — they'll hit walls. The casual pace is fine if they
genuinely enjoy each lesson; it just takes longer. We respect either.

## 11.5 · Technique check-ins (new)

The single biggest gap in an app-only piano course: nobody watches the student's
hands. Bad habits — collapsed wrists, curled pinkies, raised shoulders, thumb
pointing the wrong way — form in the first 50 lessons and become permanent.

**v1 mitigation (in the current 250-lesson plan):**

- A short **technique check-in lesson** at L5, L15, L35, L75, L125, L175, L225.
  Topics: wrist height, finger curve, hand frame, posture, thumb position.
  Cleffy demos, student watches and mirrors.
- **"Mirror Cleffy" drill** during any PLAY page: an illustrated hand shape
  appears in the corner. Student matches it before playing.
- **Honest framing on the landing page:** *"Sonata teaches reading, musicianship,
  and the Sonata method. Good physical technique is the one thing an app can only
  partly help with — practice with a mirror, or use a real teacher for occasional
  feedback if you can."*

**v2 feature (post-launch):**
- Video submission: student films a 30-second clip of their hands playing a piece.
  AI vision model flags major issues (collapsed wrist, raised shoulders, tension).
  This is a post-launch feature, not a v1 requirement.

## 11.6 · Method validation — collect data from day one (new)

"Sonata users read music faster than Simply Piano users at equivalent hours in-app"
could be the marketing line for this company for the next decade — if we have the
data. We can.

**Protocol:**

- Every 25 lessons, show a standardized sight-reading speed test:
  - 8 bars of unseen music, simple difficulty
  - Timer starts when the student sees the page
  - Record how long it takes to play it correctly (accuracy + time)
- Track results in Supabase from day one
- Compare across users, tiers, and (eventually) against competitor users

**Launch claim:** Even a sample of 20 beta testers will produce publishable results.
"In our 20-user beta, Sonata learners read sight-reading drills 1.7× faster than
users coming from apps that teach note-naming first" is a real marketing claim
if the number holds.

Beta testers become the first dataset. This starts **in month 2** with Tier 1 testers.

## 12 · What we will NOT do

These are explicit anti-patterns for Sonata. Never ship these, even
when they're tempting.

- ❌ **No mnemonics.** FACE/EGBDF are the enemy of the method.
  
  **Users will push back on this.** Many adults learned piano as kids with FACE
  and will insist it works. Have this rebuttal ready — short, confident, method-grounded:
  
  > *"We don't teach FACE or Every Good Boy Does Fine. Those mnemonics turn reading
  > into a translation step: see the note → recall the mnemonic → find the letter →
  > find the key. Sonata teaches you to see a note's shape and distance from an
  > anchor (Middle C, the G line, bass F) and go straight to the keyboard. It's one
  > step, not three. Once you're past the first tier, it's noticeably faster. Most
  > advanced pianists read this way — they just didn't start this way."*
  
  Keep this in the FAQ, the landing page, and Cleffy's onboarding. Don't cave.
- ❌ **No random sheet music with note names written under every note.** That's training wheels that never come off.
- ❌ **No emojis in Cleffy's speech.** Kills the warmth.
- ❌ **No gamification that rewards speed over understanding.** XP, streaks, badges — yes, but based on mastery passes, not raw lesson completions.
- ❌ **No ads. Ever.** Either they pay or they use the free tier. No interruptions.
- ❌ **No AI-generated covers of real pieces.** If Cleffy plays a piece, it's from a real recording or a MIDI render — not a TTS-imitating-a-piano-voice. Piano samples only.
- ❌ **No fake "social" pressure.** "12 other people are practicing right now!" — cheap. We don't do that.
- ❌ **No locked-behind-paywall beginner content.** Tiers 1 and 2 (L1–L75) should be free. You pay for the pieces you actually want to play (L76+).

## 13 · The only three questions to ask of any lesson

Every lesson we write gets checked against this.

1. **Does it use the method?** (Step / skip / leap / odd-even / intervals from anchor.)
2. **Does it respect the student?** (Not talking down. Not fake hype. Not bloated.)
3. **Does it move them one real step toward Moonlight Sonata?** (Not busywork.)

If any of the three is "no," the lesson doesn't ship.
